When Bangladesh Makes News in the West

In the past month, Bangladesh has once again found itself in the global spotlight. Student-led protests, which began in early July 2024 with demands to reform the quota system in government jobs, culminated in the resignation of Prime Minister Sheikh Hasina on August 5. On the same day, she fled the country, but not before her security forces had allegedly killed hundreds of student protesters across Bangladesh. Western media outlets scrambled to cover the dramatic turn of events, with striking photos of protesters storming the Prime Minister's residence dominating the homepages of major news outlets. While these moments of intense coverage are often crisis-driven, they also raise a broader question: What do Western media outlets talk about when they talk about Bangladesh?

Over the past decade, major Western outlets—BBC, CNN, Fox, The Telegraph, The Guardian, and The New York Times—have followed a familiar pattern when covering Bangladesh. Coverage spikes during disasters, such as the Rana Plaza factory collapse or the Rohingya refugee crisis. But outside of these crises, how is Bangladesh featured in the broader narrative? To find the answer, I analysed nearly 2,000 articles published between 2012 and 2024 to uncover the recurring themes and focal points that shape global perceptions of this evolving nation.

I focused on six major news outlets from the US and UK, selected for their high rankings on SimilarWeb . These two countries are key players in shaping global media narratives and influence millions of readers. By analysing how these outlets cover Bangladesh, we can see how the country is framed for audiences in two of the world’s largest media markets. Using "Bangladesh" as the keyword, I collected articles published between 2012 and June 2024 to understand how these media giants are shaping the conversation around the country.

SUBHEAD 1



To better understand how Western media covers Bangladesh, I analyzed nearly 2,000 articles published between 2012 and 2024 by six major news outlets. By applying machine learning techniques, I categorized these stories into themes such as politics, natural disasters, refugee crises, and labor issues. These categories reveal not just what makes the news, but also how the narratives are shaped. Moments of heightened media attention—such as the Rohingya refugee crisis or the Rana Plaza collapse—tend to drive coverage, while stories outside of these crises often reflect broader, ongoing issues that define how Bangladesh is portrayed globally.

Every dot you see represents a story about Bangladesh, arranged by the year it was published.
Now, each dot is colored based on the type of story it represents. Politics, natural disasters, refugee crises, labor issues—each color tells its own part of the story. Together, they paint a picture of how Bangladesh has been framed in global media coverage over the years.
Two moments dominate the timeline: May 2013 and July–September 2016. In May 2013, the Rana Plaza factory collapse, which claimed over 1,200 lives, brought Bangladesh into the global spotlight. Three years later, a deadly terrorist attack in Dhaka once again pushed the country to the top of the headlines. These two events marked the highest peaks of media coverage in the last decade.
Coverage is heavily skewed toward negative stories—crime, politics, and disasters dominate. Crime alone accounts for nearly 14% of all stories (265 articles), followed closely by politics (260) and sports (255). In contrast, topics like the economy, business, and culture barely make a dent. The economy, for instance, is featured in just 1.4% of the coverage (27 articles), and business even less at under 1% (18 articles). It’s clear that the focus leans toward crises rather than the everyday life or growth of the nation.

Each country has a few places that are better known, and media coverage tends to concentrate on those hotspots—often because major events happen there, or they are the capital or key economic centers. Bangladesh is no different. To explore this pattern, I used algorithms to extract every place name mentioned in the 2,000 articles and mapped them to the country’s 64 districts. My goal was to see if the media’s focus is spread across the entire country or concentrated in just a few familiar regions. This analysis helps reveal whether the coverage offers a full picture of Bangladesh or repeatedly focuses on the same areas.

The map shows the places mentioned by BBC when they talk about Bangladesh. Even though BBC has the least coverage of Dhaka compared to the other outlets, the capital still dominates, making up 65% of the mentions. Chittagong follows with 13%, and Sylhet comes in at 5%. The rest of the country? Almost entirely overlooked.
